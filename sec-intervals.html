    Section C.1 Intervals

    An interval is a subset of \(\R\) of one of the following forms, where \(a,b \in \R\) and \(a \le b\text{:}\)
    🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (a,b):=\{x\in\R \colon a\lt x\lt b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle [a,b]:=\{x\in\R \colon a\le x\le b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (a,b]:=\{x\in\R \colon a\lt x\le b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle [a,b):=\{x\in\R \colon a\le x\lt b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (a,\infty):=\{x\in\R \colon a\lt x\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle [a,\infty):=\{x\in\R \colon a\le x\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (-\infty,b):=\{x\in\R \colon x\lt b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (-\infty,b]:=\{x\in\R \colon x\le b\}\)
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle \R=(-\infty,\infty)\)
      🔗
    When \(a=b\text{,}\) the interval \((a,b)\) is the empty set and \([a,b] = \{a\}\text{.}\) A nondegenerate interval is an interval with at least two points.
    🔗
    An interval \(I\) contains every point between any two of its elements. In other words, \([x,y] \subseteq I\) whenever \(x,y \in I\) with \(x \le y\text{.}\) Conversely, any subset of \(\R\) with this property is an interval.
    🔗

    Proposition C.1.1.

    Every interval in \(\R\) is one of the nine forms listed above.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(I \subseteq \R\) be an interval. If \(I=\emptyset\text{,}\) then \(I=(0,0)\text{.}\) If \(I=\{a\}\) is a singleton, then \(I=[a,a]\text{.}\) So we may assume that \(I\) is nondegenerate.
    🔗
    First suppose that \(I\) is bounded below but not bounded above. Let \(a=\inf I\text{.}\) If \(z>a\text{,}\) then \(z\) is not a lower bound of \(I\text{,}\) so there is \(x \in I\) with \(x<z\text{.}\) Since \(I\) is not bounded above, there is also \(y \in I\) with \(z<y\text{.}\) Because \(I\) is an interval and \(x<z<y\text{,}\) we get \(z \in I\text{.}\) Thus \((a,\infty)\subseteq I\text{.}\) On the other hand, no element of \(I\) can be less than \(a\text{.}\) Hence \(I=(a,\infty)\) or \(I=[a,\infty)\text{,}\) according as \(a \notin I\) or \(a \in I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The case when \(I\) is bounded above but not bounded below is similar: if \(b=\sup I\text{,}\) then \(I=(-\infty,b)\) or \(I=(-\infty,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(I\) is neither bounded below nor bounded above, then for every \(z \in \R\) we can choose \(x,y \in I\) with \(x<z<y\text{.}\) Hence \(z \in I\text{,}\) so \(I=\R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, suppose that \(I\) is bounded below and above. Let \(a=\inf I\) and \(b=\sup I\text{.}\) Since \(I\) is nondegenerate, we have \(a<b\text{.}\) If \(a<z<b\text{,}\) then \(z\) is neither a lower bound nor an upper bound of \(I\text{.}\) So there exist \(x,y \in I\) with \(x<z<y\text{.}\) Therefore \(z \in I\text{.}\) Thus \((a,b)\subseteq I\text{.}\) Also every element of \(I\) lies in \([a,b]\text{.}\) Consequently, \(I\) is one of \((a,b), [a,b], (a,b], [a,b)\text{,}\) according to whether \(a\) and \(b\) belong to \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition C.1.2.

    Every nondegenerate interval \(I\subseteq\R\) has cardinality \(|\R|\text{.}\) In particular, it is uncountable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Every such interval \(I\) contains an open interval \((a,b)\) and is a subset of \(\R\text{.}\) It is enough to show \(|(a,b)|=|\R|\text{.}\) The map \(x\mapsto (x-c)/r\) with \(c=(a+b)/2\text{,}\) \(r=(b-a)/2\text{,}\) is a bijection \((a,b)\to(-1,1)\text{.}\) Also \(t\mapsto\tan(\pi t/2)\) is a bijection \((-1,1)\to\R\text{.}\) Hence \(|(a,b)|=|\R|\text{,}\) so \(|I|=|\R|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition C.1.3.

    The intersection of two intervals is an interval.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(I,J\) be intervals. Since \(\emptyset\) and singletons are intervals, we may assume \(I \cap J\) has at least two points \(x<y\text{.}\) Since \(I\) and \(J\) are intervals, \([x,y]\) is a subset of both \(I\) and \(J\text{,}\) and hence of \(I\cap J\text{.}\) Thus, \(I\cap J\) is an interval.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition C.1.4.

    The union of two overlapping intervals is an interval.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(I,J\) be two overlapping intervals, and pick \(a \in I \cap J\text{.}\) If \(I \cup J\) is a singleton, then we are done. Otherwise, we can take \(x \lt y\) in \(I \cup J\text{.}\) We must show \([x,y] \subseteq I \cup J\text{.}\) If both \(x\) and \(y\) lie in the same interval, then \([x,y]\) is a subset of that interval, hence of \(I \cup J\text{.}\) Otherwise, after relabeling we may assume \(x \in I \setminus J\) and \(y \in J \setminus I\text{.}\) If \(a \lt x\text{,}\) then \(x \in [a,y] \subseteq J\text{,}\) a contradiction. If \(y \lt a\text{,}\) then \(y \in [x,a] \subseteq I\text{,}\) a contradiction. Therefore \(x \le a \le y\text{.}\) Then we have \([x,a] \subseteq I\) and \([a,y] \subseteq J\text{.}\) Hence \([x,y]=[x,a]\cup[a,y]\subseteq I\cup J\text{,}\) so \(I\cup J\) is an interval.
