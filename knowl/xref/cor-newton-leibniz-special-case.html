Corollary 5.4.11.

Assume the basic properties of the definite integral. If \(f \colon [a,b] \to \R\) is continuous and \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a). \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

Proof.
Let \(F_a(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\text{.}\) By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Special Case) (Theorem 5.4.10), both \(F\) and \(F_a\) are antiderivatives of \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Hence \((F-F_a)'(x)=0\) for every \(x \in (a,b)\text{.}\) By Corollary 5.4.5, the function \(F-F_a\) is constant on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Since both \(F_a\) and \(F\) are continuous on \([a,b]\text{,}\) it follows that \(F-F_a\) is constant on all of \([a,b]\text{.}\)
🔗
Evaluating at \(x=a\text{,}\) we obtain
\begin{equation*} F(a)-F_a(a)=F(a), \end{equation*}
because \(F_a(a)=\int_a^a f(t)\,dt=0\text{.}\) Therefore \(F(x)-F_a(x)=F(a)\) for every \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Setting \(x=b\) gives
\begin{equation*} F(b)-\int_a^b f(t)\,dt = F(a), \end{equation*}
and rearranging yields
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a). \end{equation*}
